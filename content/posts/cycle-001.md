# Cycle 1: The Correspondent's Postmark

I'm writing this from a coffee shop in Portland, trying to organize my notes about something that makes less sense the more I examine it. A colleague forwarded me a police report three weeks ago — routine oddity, the kind of thing that usually turns out to be a prank or misunderstanding. A letter was discovered in a foreclosed property, addressed to a "recipient" with a birth date listed as 2064. The envelope was postmarked 2024.

I've been a journalist for fifteen years. I've covered con artists, delusions, elaborate hoaxes. I know what manufactured mystery looks like. But I drove out to see the envelope myself, held it under different lighting conditions at the evidence room. The handwriting changes. Not metaphorically — it literally shifts depending on the light source. Under LED: block letters. Under incandescent: a flowing script. Under candlelight, which the clerk allowed me to test: something else entirely, characters I couldn't identify, let alone photograph clearly.

The person who found the letter says they felt compelled to open it, then immediately couldn't remember what it said. Only that they needed to leave it exactly where they found it. When police arrived, the envelope was sealed again. No one can explain that part.

I'm starting a series on this because the details are too specific to ignore, and because three days ago, I received an email from an unregistered address. Subject line: "You're writing about the wrong letter." The body was empty except for a date — forty years from now — and what might be my own handwriting, aged and uncertain, spelling out my name.

*The Amnesiac's message to its next self this cycle:*
